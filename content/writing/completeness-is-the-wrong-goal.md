When a system collects data, the default instinct is to collect all of it. More coverage feels safer, more thorough, more defensible in a review. It is also, for anything with a real per-unit cost, the wrong objective.

## Volume is not value

Not all coverage is worth the same. Some of what you collect changes decisions; some of it is looked at by no one. A segment can be tiny in volume and decisive in impact, or enormous in volume and irrelevant. A metric that counts units treats these identically, which means optimizing it sends effort exactly where it isn't needed.

## Tie collection to utilization

The fix is to measure not just coverage — how much of a thing you see — but utilization: how much of what you see actually feeds a decision. The gap between the two is where money is being spent for nothing.

Once you can see that gap, the allocation argument changes character. "Should we collect more of X" stops being a matter of who argues hardest and becomes a question with an answer: does an additional unit of X change a decision often enough to justify its cost.

## The framework is the leverage

The deliverable here is not a one-time reallocation. It's a shared way of judging every future request. Before the framework, saying no to a low-value collection request is political. After it, it's obvious — and obvious no's are much easier to hold.

The long tail doesn't disappear. It just gets treated honestly, as an ROI question rather than a completeness obligation.
